Why this programme?

Digital medicine and smart healthcare will not be realised without the cognizant participation of clinicians in the entire development process and cycle. Clinicians educated in these aspects provide invaluable insights during the design stage of AI-enabled tools — ensuring solutions meet clinical needs, emphasise patient safety, and integrate into clinical workflows.

This programme is built on a peer-reviewed integrative review (Chew & Ngiam, BMC Medicine 2025) and the supplementary materials that accompany it. It is the second in a three-part series: after AI-Ready Clinician, and before AI-Savvy Clinician.

Key message from the source paper
The AI healthcare market was valued at USD 20.9 billion in 2024 and is growing at 48.1% annually. 79% of healthcare organisations are already using AI. ROI is realised within 14 months, generating USD 3.20 for every USD 1 invested. The question is not whether AI will touch your practice — it already has.

Step 1 — Clinical problem identification
Real and solvable clinical problems with availability of data. Deciding the level (general use, fine-tuned, or new model) and the type of algorithm needed.
  • Is this a descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, or prescriptive problem?
  • Is the problem about signs (vision), symptoms (NLP), or actions (robotics)?
  • Could a simpler rule-based system solve it instead?
Clinician as problem-owner
Step 2 — Form team or collaborate
Consisting of people with the necessary skill sets: data scientists, data engineers, data architects, chief data officer, health informatics experts, clinicians, health administrators, and patient groups.
  • The ideal team for a new model from scratch includes 12 distinct roles — from ML engineer to QA tester to clinical champion.
  • The clinician's irreplaceable role: domain expert, problem definer, data annotator, and real-world validator.
Clinician as domain expert
Step 3 — Data organisation & curation
The right and quality data to be explored, annotated, and pre-processed: standardisation for interoperability, cataloguing, and de-identification.
  • Clinicians are the only people who can judge whether a label is clinically meaningful.
  • Data without infrastructure = generic AI. Data with curated, rich, interoperable metadata = personalised AI.
Clinician as data guardian
Step 4 — Infrastructure & architecture
Capacity of computers and servers necessary to operate huge amounts of data at optimal speed. Cloud vs on-premise vs federated learning.
  • The AI system pipeline runs in three stages: input (data feeds, APIs), processing (model execution, MLOps), output (chatbots, dashboards, tracking tools).
  • Clinicians need to ask: where does patient data go? Who can access it? What happens if the system fails?
Clinician as infrastructure questioner
Step 5 — Explore AI neural networks
Find tested AI algorithms available on open access platforms — or create anew. Three levels of AI technology usage: general use (zero-shot prompting), fine-tuned model (adapting to a task), and new model from scratch.
  • Creating a new LLM from scratch requires hundreds of high-end GPUs running for weeks — extremely expensive.
  • Most clinical use cases are better served by fine-tuning or RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation).
Clinician as intelligent commissioner
Step 6 — Validate AI/ML models
Proper clinical or non-clinical validation, clinical trials or equivalent to estimate effect in the real-world settings.
  • Key metrics: sensitivity, specificity, AUC, PPV, NPV, F1 score, calibration.
  • Fairness and subgroup performance — does the model work equally well for your patient population?
  • Frameworks: TRIPOD-AI, CONSORT-AI, DECIDE-AI, MI-CLAIM, APPRAISE-AI.
Clinician as evaluator
Step 7 — Registration
Register tools with the relevant authority (medical device authority) to market for broader use and application.
  • FDA has cleared over 1,000 predominantly radiological AI tools.
  • Key distinction: diagnostic/treatment AI is under FDA purview. Administrative AI, wellness apps, and general LLMs are not.
  • Malaysia: check MOH and MDA guidance; MMC Guideline on Ethical Use of AI in Medical Practice (2025) applies.
Clinician as regulatory voice
Step 8 — Deployment & monitoring
Deployment strategy that considers the software systems or applications environment, user interfaces of alerts or notifications that achieve practice efficiency and provider acceptance and adoption.
  • Alert fatigue is a real risk — the design of how AI outputs are presented matters as much as their accuracy.
  • AI tools can be copilots (real-time), asynchronous (offline analysis), or autonomous (end-to-end).
  • Post-deployment: watch for model drift as patient populations and clinical practice change.
Clinician as monitor
Step 9 — Improve the AI ecosystem
Active and continuous engagement with all stakeholders including the public to improve confidence and to progress the AI ecosystem.
  • The AI ecosystem has 8 components: data quality, technology infrastructure, talent, regulatory frameworks, industry collaboration, market investment, user feedback, and risk management.
  • Clinician feedback loops are the most underused and most valuable input for ecosystem improvement.
Clinician as advocate

Session 1 · 75 min · Discussion-led

Why AI? Identifying clinical problems
and building the right team

Understand what kinds of problems are genuinely suitable for AI, what different levels of AI use look like, and what role you play in the development team.

Steps 1 & 2 Clinician as problem-owner

The AI landscape — from narrow AI to AGI

AI is the cognitive ability of machines made possible by mathematically designed neural networks. The hierarchy runs from AI (mathematical algorithms) → ML (self-learning algorithms) → DL (multiple neural networks, e.g. NLP, computer vision, generative AI) → AGI (hypothetical human-level general intelligence). Crucially, Augmented Intelligence (AugI) supplements and enhances human ability rather than substituting it — this is the most clinically relevant framing.

Three levels of AI usage — where do you sit?

Level 1 — General use (zero-shot)
Using existing AI models with prompting. Examples: Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini for clinical writing, summarising guidelines, drafting referrals. No infrastructure needed. This is where most clinicians start — and where prompt engineering matters.
Level 2 — Fine-tuned model
Adapting an existing model to a specific clinical task or dataset. Examples: BioBERT for biomedical NLP, MedGemma for medical image and text, AI diagnosis assistants trained on local EMR data. Requires data and technical collaboration — clinician defines the problem and validates outputs.
Level 3 — New model from scratch
Creating a new AI model from scratch. Examples: AlphaFold (protein folding), GPT/LLaMA trained from massive corpora, pretraining models on de-identified EMR data across hospitals. Extremely expensive — requires hundreds of GPUs running for weeks. Rare in clinical settings; usually requires a funded research programme.
The "crossing the chasm" challenge
Moving from general AI use to personalised AI requires crossing six chasms: weak digital infrastructure, scarcity of local data, workforce skill shortages, financial constraints, governance gaps, and adoption challenges. Understanding which chasm your institution is stuck in shapes which problems are currently AI-solvable.

Identifying the right problem

  • Descriptive — patient profiling, disease patterns (past/present)
  • Diagnostic — early detection, risk assessment (present)
  • Predictive — complication risk prediction, treatment response (future)
  • Prescriptive — personalised treatment plans, behavioural interventions (future, highest value)
  • Signs — vision intelligence: image recognition, radiology, pathology (e.g. ECgMLP achieving 99.26% accuracy in endometrial cancer detection)
  • Symptoms — NLP: summarisation, classification, extraction, translation, conversation in clinical contexts
  • Actions — robotics, procedural assistance, autonomous systems

The ideal development team

Building a new AI model from scratch requires at minimum 12 roles: Project Lead/Clinical Champion, Product Manager, AI/ML Engineer, Data Scientists, Lead Software Architect, UI/UX Designer, Backend Developer, Frontend Developer, Mobile App Developer, AI Integration Specialist/Prompt Engineer, QA Engineer, and Clinical Experts/Advisors. The clinician is both the clinical champion and the domain expert advisor — indispensable at every stage.

ReflectionSession 1 — Check your understanding
A colleague wants to build an AI tool that predicts which patients with type 2 diabetes are at high risk of hospitalisation in the next 12 months, using existing EMR data. Which level of AI development does this most likely require?
Level 1 — General use with prompting only
Level 2 — A fine-tuned model trained on local EMR data
Level 3 — A new model built from scratch, like GPT
This is a rule-based system, not AI
Correct
A predictive risk model using existing patient data is a classic fine-tuning task. A general LLM can't do this without training on local data — but building from scratch would be disproportionately expensive. The clinician's role here is to define "hospitalisation risk", identify which variables matter clinically, and validate outputs against real patient outcomes.
Not quite
Predicting patient risk from EMR data requires a model trained on local, labelled clinical data — that's fine-tuning. General prompting can't access your EMR, and building from scratch would be unnecessary and extremely expensive. The predictive problem type sits in the future quadrant of the Descriptive → Diagnostic → Predictive → Prescriptive framework.

Session 2 · 75 min · Concept + case-based

Data, infrastructure
and building the model

What makes clinical data suitable, how annotation and labelling works, what infrastructure means in practice, and how to critically assess the foundations of any AI tool.

Steps 3, 4 & 5 Clinician as data guardian

What makes data suitable for AI?

  • Quality — accurate, complete, consistent, with meaningful labels annotated by clinicians
  • Quantity — sufficient volume to train a generalisable model
  • Representativeness — reflects your patient population, not just the populations of Western academic centres
  • Interoperability — standardised for linkage across systems (HL7 FHIR, SNOMED CT, ICD-10/11)
  • De-identification — compliant with PDPA and institutional data governance requirements
The data guardian principle
General AI (without supportive infrastructure) uses generic, uncurated, or sparse data → produces generic suggestions with limited relevance. Personalised AI (with infrastructure) uses curated, rich, interoperable data with metadata → produces context-aware, actionable recommendations. Clinicians are the only people who can judge whether labels are clinically meaningful and whether the data truly represents their patients.

Learning types — what clinicians need to know

Supervised learning
Trained on labelled data. The model learns to map inputs to outputs. Most clinical prediction models use this — and clinicians are the labellers. Your annotation decisions determine model quality.
Unsupervised learning
No predefined labels. The model finds patterns. Useful for patient clustering, anomaly detection, discovering disease subtypes you didn't know existed.
Reinforcement learning
An agent learns by receiving rewards or penalties. Used in robotics, game AI, and increasingly in clinical decision optimisation (e.g. personalised treatment dose adjustment).
Transfer & federated learning
Transfer learning reuses a model trained on one task for another. Federated learning trains locally on each device — only model updates are shared, not patient data. This is the privacy-preserving approach hospitals should demand.

Programmed systems vs true AI — the critical distinction

Many tools described as "AI" are actually rule-based programmed systems. The distinction matters because clinicians should apply different levels of scrutiny. Rule-based systems are transparent and predictable; true AI systems learn from data and may behave unexpectedly outside their training distribution.

Key distinction: programmed vs AI

Drug-drug interaction checkerDL model predicting adverse drug reactions

ReflectionSession 2 — Check your understanding
A hospital wants to train an AI model to detect diabetic retinopathy from retinal photographs. The data science team says they have 50,000 retinal images but none are labelled. What is the most important contribution clinicians can make at this stage?
Decide which neural network architecture to use
Annotate the images with clinically meaningful labels and define the grading criteria
Increase the dataset size by collecting more images first
Ensure the images are de-identified — that is the only clinical input needed
Exactly right
Without clinician annotation, 50,000 images are just unlabelled data. The model learns to predict the label you give it — which means clinically meaningful, consistent, and well-defined labels are the foundation of model quality. Neural network architecture is a data scientist decision. De-identification is necessary but not sufficient. Volume matters less than label quality.
Not quite
The most critical bottleneck here is annotation — supervised learning requires labelled data, and only clinicians can provide clinically meaningful labels. Neural network choice, de-identification, and dataset size are all important, but none of them substitute for the clinical expertise needed to grade retinal images accurately and consistently.

Session 3 · 75 min · Framework-focused

Validating, evaluating
and registering an AI tool

The metrics clinicians must understand, the frameworks that govern AI reporting, and the registration pathways — including what is and isn't under regulatory control in Malaysia and globally.

Steps 6 & 7 Clinician as evaluator

The validation ladder

  • Internal validation — tested on the same data distribution it was trained on. Necessary but not sufficient.
  • External validation — tested on data from a different institution or time period. Essential before clinical use.
  • Prospective validation — tested on new patients as they present. The gold standard.
  • Sandboxing — safe pre-deployment testing in real-life settings without affecting critical care decisions.

Metrics clinicians must demand

Sensitivity & specificity
How often does the model correctly identify disease (sensitivity) and correctly exclude it (specificity)? The trade-off between these is context-dependent — a screening tool prioritises sensitivity; a confirmatory test prioritises specificity.
AUC (Area Under Curve)
Overall discriminative ability. AUC of 0.5 = random; 1.0 = perfect. Above 0.8 is generally considered good clinical performance — but AUC alone tells you nothing about calibration.
Calibration
Does a predicted 70% risk actually correspond to 70% of patients having the outcome? A well-calibrated model is essential for clinical decision-making. Most papers report AUC without calibration — ask for it.
Subgroup performance & fairness
Does the model perform equally well across age groups, ethnic groups, and comorbidities? AI trained predominantly on non-local populations may perform worse for your patients. Always ask: who was in the training data?
The transparency toolkit — overcoming the black box
SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) — how much each clinical variable influenced the prediction. LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations) — why the AI made this specific decision for this patient. Grad-CAM — highlights which regions of a medical image the AI focused on. Clinicians should demand these tools for any AI used in high-stakes decisions.

Registration — what is and isn't regulated

Under FDA regulatory purview
Machines for diagnosing, treating, or preventing disease. Over 1,000 predominantly radiological AI tools have been cleared. Some health care apps. Software that fits the definition of a medical device.
NOT under FDA regulatory purview
Generative AI models broadly applicable to multiple tasks (including ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini). Administrative software. Patient-facing mental health wellness apps. Tools for hospital resource allocation. Software where clinicians will not primarily rely on recommendations. This gap has significant implications for accountability.
Malaysia — MMC Guideline 2025
The Malaysian Medical Council has issued a Guideline on the Ethical Use of AI in Medical Practice (Version 1/2025). Registered medical practitioners continue to be bound by MMC's Code of Professional Conduct. They must develop skills to apply AI critically, evaluate recommendations based on professional clinical insight, and communicate AI benefits, risks, and limitations to facilitate informed consent. ReflectionSession 3 — Check your understanding
A vendor presents an AI sepsis prediction tool with an AUC of 0.91, validated on 200,000 patients from three US academic medical centres. You are considering deploying it in your Malaysian district hospital. What is your most important question?
Is the AUC above 0.9? If yes, the tool is clinically excellent.
Has this been published in a peer-reviewed journal?
How does it perform in populations demographically similar to our patients, and has it been externally validated in a Southeast Asian setting?
Is it FDA-cleared?
Excellent clinical thinking
High AUC in a US academic centre does not translate directly to performance in a Malaysian district hospital where patient demographics, comorbidity profiles, and clinical workflows differ significantly. External validation in a similar population is the critical question. FDA clearance and peer-reviewed publication are important but secondary to fitness for your specific context.
Look again
The key issue here is population generalisability. An excellent AUC in a US academic centre tells you little about performance in Malaysian district hospitals. Publication and FDA clearance matter — but a tool can have both and still perform poorly in your population. Always ask: where was it validated, and how similar is that population to mine?

Session 4 · 75 min · Governance capstone

Deployment, monitoring
and the AI ecosystem

How AI tools enter clinical workflows, why models degrade, and what clinicians and administrators must do to maintain safe, effective, equitable AI in their institutions.

Steps 8 & 9 Clinician as monitor & advocate

Challenges in healthcare AI — the four domains

1. Development to deployment — TEAM
Without the right multidisciplinary team from the start, AI tools that technically work may fail clinically. Clinicians who are not involved early are then asked to adopt tools that don't fit their workflow.
2. Data quality and security — INFRASTRUCTURE
AI without supportive data infrastructure produces generic, low-relevance outputs. Secure, standardised, accessible data is the foundation — and the biggest gap in most healthcare settings.
3. Integration in clinical workflows — AWARENESS
Alert fatigue, poor UI design, and lack of training lead to tools being ignored or misused. Clinical champions who understand both the tool and the workflow are essential for adoption.
4. Transparency and accountability — POLICY
Without clear governance, liability is diffuse. When AI-assisted decisions cause harm, who is responsible? Current legal and regulatory frameworks are still catching up — clinician advocacy for policy is urgent.
The next and ultimate challenge is our creativity
Team, infrastructure, awareness, and policy are solvable problems. But the organisations that will truly benefit from AI are those that build practical "testing grounds" for real hands-on practice — and that allow clinicians to run with their insights. Data has to be king: secure yet accessible, standardised across departments, and open for experimentation.

Source: Howard Yu, March 2025 ↗

The AI ecosystem — 8 components

  • Data quality and availability — high-quality, diverse, privacy-protected sources
  • Technology infrastructure — hardware, software frameworks, development tools
  • Talent and expertise — data scientists, engineers, clinical domain experts
  • Regulatory and ethical frameworks — clear guidelines, accountability mechanisms
  • Industry collaboration — partnerships across hospitals, universities, industry
  • Market demand and investment — funding and institutional support
  • User adoption and feedback loop — clinician engagement informs AI improvement
  • Risk management and resilience — proactive cybersecurity, incident pathways

ReflectionSession 4 — Capstone
Six months after deploying an AI early warning system for acute kidney injury, nurses report that the alert fires so frequently that most staff have stopped acting on it. The sensitivity is high but clinicians are ignoring it. Which concept best explains this failure, and what domain does it belong to?
Hallucination — the AI is generating incorrect outputs (Model failure)
Training data bias — the model wasn't trained on enough AKI cases (Data failure)
Alert fatigue — too many alerts desensitise clinicians, undermining even accurate outputs (Deployment failure)
Automation bias — clinicians are over-trusting the AI (Awareness failure)
Well reasoned
Alert fatigue is a deployment and integration failure — it has nothing to do with model accuracy. A highly sensitive model that fires too often creates a "cry wolf" effect, ultimately making the system less safe than no system at all. The fix requires redesigning the alert interface, adjusting threshold triggers, and involving clinicians in the deployment design from the start. This is squarely in the Integration domain: AWARENESS.
Look again
The model itself may be working correctly — high sensitivity means it's catching AKI cases. The failure is in how the output is delivered and received. When alerts are too frequent, clinicians become desensitised — this is alert fatigue, a well-documented deployment integration problem. It belongs in the AWARENESS domain of the four-challenge framework.

Programme closing takeaways

  • AI tool development is a cyclical 9-step process — clinician involvement is required at every step, not just at the end.
  • The most important thing a clinician brings to AI development is domain expertise, clinical judgement, and accountability — none of which can be automated.
  • Understanding this process helps you codesign, evaluate, monitor, and regulate AI tools more effectively than any clinician who remains uninformed.
  • Appropriate AI in healthcare is ethical, clinically validated, and seamlessly integrated — all three conditions must be met, not just one.
  • "AI only enhances who you already are, or wanted to be." — the question is whether you build the skills to direct it.
